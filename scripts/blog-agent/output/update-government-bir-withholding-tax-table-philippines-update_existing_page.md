## BIR Withholding Tax Table 2026 (Compensation Income)

The BIR prescribes different withholding tax tables depending on your payroll frequency. Below are the tax schedules applicable in 2026 for **monthly** and **semi-monthly** payroll periods. These are derived from the annual tax table under the TRAIN Law (effective January 1, 2023 onward).

> ⚠️ **Important:** These tables are provided for reference and estimation purposes only. They are not an official BIR document. Verify with your employer's payroll team or directly at [bir.gov.ph](https://www.bir.gov.ph) before filing or relying on these figures for official use.

| Annual Taxable Income | Income Tax Due |
|---|---|
| ₱250,000 and below | ₱0 (exempt) |
| Over ₱250,000 to ₱400,000 | 15% of the excess over ₱250,000 |
| Over ₱400,000 to ₱800,000 | ₱22,500 + 20% of the excess over ₱400,000 |
| Over ₱800,000 to ₱2,000,000 | ₱102,500 + 25% of the excess over ₱800,000 |

| Over ₱8,000,000 | ₱2,202,500 + 35% of the excess over ₱8,000,000 |

*Source: Republic Act No. 10963 (TRAIN Law), as implemented by BIR Revenue Regulation No. 8-2018.*

## How to Compute Monthly Withholding Tax in the Philippines (2026)

### The Formula

```
Withholding Tax = Base Tax + (Taxable Compensation − Income Bracket Floor) × Applicable Rate
```

**Taxable Compensation** is computed as:

```
Gross Monthly Salary
− Non-taxable allowances (e.g., de minimis benefits within BIR limits)
− SSS employee contribution
− PhilHealth employee contribution
− Pag-IBIG employee contribution
= Taxable Compensation
```

---

### Example 1: Monthly Payroll (Mid-Range Salary)

**Employee profile:**
- Gross monthly salary: ₱35,000
- SSS contribution: ₱1,350 (estimated; verify current SSS table)
- PhilHealth contribution: ₱875 (estimated at current rate; verify with PhilHealth)
- Pag-IBIG contribution: ₱100
- No other non-taxable deductions

**Step 1 — Compute taxable compensation:**

```
₱35,000 − ₱1,350 − ₱875 − ₱100 = ₱32,675
```

**Step 2 — Identify the correct bracket (Monthly Table):**

₱32,675 falls in the bracket: **Over ₱20,833 to ₱33,333**
- Base tax: ₱0
- Rate: 15% of the excess over ₱20,833

**Step 3 — Compute the withholding tax:**

```
Excess = ₱32,675 − ₱20,833 = ₱11,842
Withholding Tax = 15% × ₱11,842 = ₱1,776.30
```

**Monthly withholding tax: ₱1,776.30**

---

### Example 2: Monthly Payroll (Higher Salary)

**Employee profile:**
- Gross monthly salary: ₱80,000
- SSS contribution: ₱1,350
- PhilHealth contribution: ₱2,000 (estimated)
- Pag-IBIG contribution: ₱100

**Step 1 — Compute taxable compensation:**

```
₱80,000 − ₱1,350 − ₱2,000 − ₱100 = ₱76,550
```

**Step 2 — Identify the correct bracket (Monthly Table):**

₱76,550 falls in: **Over ₱66,667 to ₱166,667**
- Base tax: ₱8,542
- Rate: 25% of the excess over ₱66,667

**Step 3 — Compute the withholding tax:**

```
Excess = ₱76,550 − ₱66,667 = ₱9,883
Withholding Tax = ₱8,542 + (25% × ₱9,883)
               = ₱8,542 + ₱2,470.75
               = ₱11,012.75
```

**Monthly withholding tax: ₱11,012.75**

### Example 3: Semi-Monthly Payroll

**Employee profile:**
- Gross semi-monthly salary: ₱18,000
- SSS contribution: ₱675
- PhilHealth contribution: ₱450
- Pag-IBIG contribution: ₱100

**Step 1 — Compute taxable compensation:**

```
₱18,000 − ₱675 − ₱450 − ₱100 = ₱16,775
```

**Step 2 — Identify the correct bracket (Semi-Monthly Table):**

₱16,775 falls in: **Over ₱16,667 to ₱33,333**
- Base tax: ₱937.50
- Rate: 20% of the excess over ₱16,667

**Step 3 — Compute:**

```
Excess = ₱16,775 − ₱16,667 = ₱108
Withholding Tax = ₱937.50 + (20% × ₱108)
               = ₱937.50 + ₱21.60
               = ₱959.10
```

**Semi-monthly withholding tax: ₱959.10**

## Withholding Tax Rates: Before and After TRAIN Law

A common question is whether the withholding tax table has changed for 2026. Here is a quick comparison of where things stand.

| Period | Tax-Exempt Threshold | Top Rate | Key Law |

**What changed in Phase 2 (2023)?** The middle tax brackets were reduced. For example, the rate on income between ₱250,001 and ₱400,000 annually dropped from 20% to 15%. This means most middle-income employees pay less tax starting 2023.

## Frequently Asked Questions: Withholding Tax in the Philippines (2026)

---

**Has the BIR withholding tax table changed for 2026?**

As of this writing, no new BIR Revenue Regulation has amended the withholding tax table for compensation income in 2026. The table currently in force is the one that took effect on January 1, 2023 under the second tranche of TRAIN Law rate reductions. Always check bir.gov.ph or ask your payroll department for any mid-year updates.

---

**What is the tax-exempt threshold in 2026?**

Employees earning ₱250,000 or less in annual taxable compensation income are exempt from income tax. On a monthly basis, this works out to approximately ₱20,833. If your monthly taxable compensation is at or below this amount, no withholding tax is deducted.

---

**What is included in taxable compensation?**

Taxable compensation includes your basic salary, regular allowances, and other taxable benefits. It does not include your mandatory SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG contributions (which are deducted first), non-taxable de minimis benefits within BIR-prescribed limits, and the 13th month pay and other benefits up to ₱90,000 per year.

---

**Does the ₱90,000 13th month pay exemption still apply in 2026?**

Yes. Under the TRAIN Law, 13th month pay and other benefits are exempt from income tax up to ₱90,000 per year. Amounts above ₱90,000 are added to taxable compensation and subject to withholding tax.

---

**My employer uses a semi-monthly payroll. Which table applies?**

Use the semi-monthly withholding tax table. The BIR prescribes separate tables for different payroll frequencies — monthly, semi-monthly, weekly, and daily — so that the annual tax liability is correctly distributed across pay periods. See the semi-monthly table above for the 2026 figures.

---

**Why does my withholding tax differ from the amount I see in a calculator?**

Withholding tax estimates can differ due to variables like non-taxable allowances, de minimis benefits, additional compensation (bonuses, overtime), or your employer's specific payroll setup. The BIR withholding tax calculator and the tables on this page give estimates. Your official tax liability is determined when you file your annual income tax return (BIR Form 1700 or 1701A, as applicable).

---

**Where can I verify the official BIR withholding tax table?**

The official tables are published by the BIR at [bir.gov.ph](https://www.bir.gov.ph). The applicable Revenue Regulations for the current schedule are RR No. 8-2018 and subsequent amending issuances. You can also ask your HR or payroll team to provide a copy of the BIR-prescribed withholding tax table applicable to your payroll period.

---

**How do I compute my take-home pay after withholding tax?**

Your take-home pay is your gross salary minus all mandatory deductions (SSS, PhilHealth, Pag-IBIG) and your withholding tax, minus any other voluntary deductions. Use the **[Take-Home Pay Calculator](/calculators/tax/take-home-pay-calculator-philippines)** for a complete breakdown, or read the **[take-home pay guide](/guides/tax/take-home-pay-guide-philippines)** for a full explanation of all deductions.
